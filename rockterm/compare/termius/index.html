Where RockTerm Pulls Ahead

Built for Network Engineers

12 built-in network tools (subnet calc, route summarization, prefix-list generator, regex lab, and more) that Termius simply doesn't have. You don't need a second app for basic network math.

Real Scripting Engine

Expect-style automation with Python support, a script recorder, syntax-highlighted editor, and triggers. Termius has snippets. RockTerm has a full programming environment for terminal automation.

7 File Transfer Protocols

SFTP, Zmodem (auto-detect), Xmodem, Ymodem, ASCII, TFTP server, and drag-and-drop upload. Termius has SFTP. If you've ever needed to push firmware to a switch over TFTP, you know this matters.

Your Data Never Leaves

No cloud vault. No sync service. No telemetry. Credentials are encrypted locally with AES. Termius syncs your data through their servers — fine for some, a non-starter for others.

Professional Session Logging

Log rotation by date and size, dynamic filename templates, auto-start on connect, ANSI stripping, and command-line log flags. Built for environments where audit trails are required, not optional.

You Own Your AI

RockTerm's AI uses your own API key (Claude or GPT), is disabled by default, and gives you a full chat panel with context selection. Termius bakes AI into the product with no provider choice. AI usage is billed directly by your chosen provider, not by Rock River Research.

What about cross-platform and cloud sync?

Termius runs on macOS, Linux, iOS, and Android in addition to Windows. If you need to SSH from your phone or sync sessions across a MacBook and an iPad, Termius does that. It also supports Mosh for high-latency connections and offers real-time session sharing for team collaboration.

RockTerm is Windows-only and keeps everything local — and that's a deliberate security decision, not a missing feature. Here's why:

Cloud-synced credentials are a high-value target. When a vendor stores your SSH keys, passwords, and session bookmarks on their servers, a single breach exposes every connection you've ever saved — across every device. This isn't hypothetical; credential-syncing services have been breached before, and terminal emulators hold the keys to production infrastructure.

RockTerm's local-only architecture aligns with real-world security standards and best practices:

  • NIST SP 800-53 (SC-28, SC-12) — Recommends protecting credentials at rest and minimizing key distribution. Local-only storage eliminates the third-party sync vector entirely.
  • CIS Controls v8 (Control 6) — Calls for centralized credential management under your organization's control, not a SaaS vendor's cloud.
  • Zero Trust Architecture (NIST SP 800-207) — A core Zero Trust principle: minimize implicit trust in third-party intermediaries. Your SSH keys should not transit or reside on infrastructure you don't own.
  • SOC 2 / ISO 27001 — Both frameworks require documented control over sensitive credential storage. Local storage with AES encryption under a master password you control is straightforward to audit and certify.
  • PCI DSS Requirement 8 — Mandates strong access-credential management. Syncing credentials to a vendor cloud introduces a shared-responsibility gap that complicates PCI compliance.

RockTerm encrypts your credential vault locally with AES under a master password that never leaves your machine. No cloud account, no sync servers, no vendor breach surface. If your security posture matters — and if you're managing network infrastructure, it does — keeping credentials local isn't a limitation. It's the point.
